 Dungeons and Dragons (Fifth Edition) is a tabletop roleplaying game, in which every player brings their own created character to the table. The first two identifying choices one makes for any character are the character class - which determines abilites and usually plays into a specific fantasy trope - and character race, which determines appearance, some abilities, and also often plays into a fantasy trope. Since these are decisions everyone must make for every character, and since it's a collaborative game in which balance among players is important, you'll often find rather heated fights online about it! Which classes are the best or most popular? Are any races way ahead of the others in power? Why do martial characters suck so much more than magical characters? It's certainly a topic with strong opinions, which can often be found clogging multiple D&D-dedicated Reddit boards. The data for these visualizations was taken from one such board, post found here.
 For this assignment, we seek to argue the specific question: Is any one race clearly better (more popular) than the others?

Humans are either the first or second most picked race for every class except Barbarian.
 On the right, we argue that humans are the most popular race choice by far. We do this by showing the top two races picked for each class - a breakdown where humans are disproportionately represented. By showing both the two most popular choices, we get humans on the chart for nearly every class, making their color (chosen to be the darkest on the chart) dominate the graph. The human lines are also .3 pixels thicker than all others, to draw the eye and make their color further dominate the chart, without being openly manipulative. A lollipop chart is chosen to make easy comparisons between lines, as humans are pretty good at judging distance, and to take up less of the limited x-axis space than a bar chart would. Color is the channel for racial attribute to impress on the viewer at a glance that one race is clearly the most popular - and a key is given to make sure the viewer realizes who the color belongs to.
 On the left, we argue that no one race is substantially more popular or unbalanced than the others. We do this by showing the breakdown of the most popular classes for each race, where four races (elves, humans, half-elves, and dwarves) look nearly similar. The circle-size channel is used to make the largest four look more similar than they really are, as the human eye is pretty bad at differentiating circle size. The animation is chosen for the same reason - to further confuse comparison. A color key is omitted in favor of a tooltip to obfuscate the fact that each race is showing only one of its fifteen possible combinations, and that these are not total numbers. The caption offers an explanation for the proportionally huge number of smaller circles - "well, they cost more!" - which allows the viewer to rationalize and move on from that as a discrepancy.
